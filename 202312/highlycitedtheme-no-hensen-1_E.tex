This section compares changes in highly cited themes with the cited themes of non–highly cited papers.
Table \ref{tab:topmesh} shows the most frequent cited themes and citing themes for each cumulative cohort and each group. Among these, variation is observed only in the cited themes of G1 (i.e., highly cited themes).

Next, to examine the diversity of cited and citing themes in each cumulative cohort, changes in entropy were analyzed for each group. The entropy of the occurrence counts of cited themes, citing themes, and cited–citing theme pairs is shown in Figure \ref{fig:arr2}. For counting, fractional counting per paper is used for both cited and citing themes. For cited–citing theme pairs, a bipartite graph is constructed for each reference, and fractional counting is applied such that the total weight of edges sums to one. Figure \ref{fig:arr2} shows that only highly cited papers exhibit low entropy, while there is little difference among the other groups. Note that the cumulative cohort for 1946–1950 is excluded due to insufficient assignment of MeSH descriptors.

Furthermore, researchers’ citation behavior does not necessarily involve citing papers within the same theme. To assess the degree of such thematic shifts, the cosine distance between cited and citing themes was examined for each group. The results are shown in Figure \ref{fig:meshpivot}. Again, only highly cited papers exhibit a distinct pattern. Moreover, for highly cited papers, the degree of thematic shift appears to decrease after the cumulative cohort of 1946–2010, suggesting that differences from other groups are diminishing.
